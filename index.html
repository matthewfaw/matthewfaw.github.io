About Me

I am a fourth-year Ph.D. student in the ECE department at The University of Texas at Austin, where I am very fortunate to be advised by Sanjay Shakkottai and Constantine Caramanis.

Prior to joining UT Austin, I spent four fun years at Duke University, where I graduated with majors in Electrical & Computer Engineering, Computer Science, and Mathematics. During my time there, I had the great fortune of working with Ben Lee on designing economically sustainable algorithms for datacenter-level computational sprinting, Richard Fair on designing digital microfluidic devices capable of manipulating cells within a droplet, and Nick Buchler on using CRISPR technologies to create cells which perform logical operations.

Outside of school, I've also spent time in industry. During the summer of 2016, I was a software engineering intern at MathWorks on the Stateflow Semantics team, where I developed a new code generation framework. Between undergrad and grad school, I also spent a year working as a software engineer at a healthcare software startup called Verato, where I co-developed a continuous integration platform on Kubernetes and wrote code to maintain and update the identity data in the company's main database.

Research Interests

My interest lie broadly in the design and analysis of online algorithms and learning problems in information-constrained and dynamic environments. I am particularly interested in studying the power and limitations of adaptive algorithms, and in understanding the settings where adaptivity gives provable performance guarantees over non-adaptive algorithms. These interests have lead me to study a variety of problems, ranging from variations of multi-armed bandits, (limited information) prophet inequalities, (adaptive) optimization algorithms, and domain adaptation problems.

  • Single Sample Prophet Inequalities via Greedy-Ordered Selection (supersedes these two papers).
    Constantine Caramanis, Paul Duetting, Matthew Faw, Federico Fusco, Philip Lazos, Stefano Leonardi, Orestis Papadigenopoulos, and Emmanouil Pountourakis, Rebecca Reiffenhauser (Alphabetical order)
    ACM-SIAM Symposium on Discrete Algorithms, SODA'22, Alexandria, VA, USA.

    Background/Setup:
    • We consider the problem of designing prophet inequalities in limited information regimes under combinatorial constraints.
    • The prophet inequality problem is an optimal stopping problem. In the problem, a gambler is presented with a sequence of non-negative random variables, one at a time. At each time, the gambler may either collect this reward, or forfeit this reward forever. Each reward is associated with some element in a combinatorial constraint set (e.g., an element in a matroid, or an edge in a matching on a graph), and the gambler can only collect sets of elements which are feasible in this constraint set. The game stops when either the gambler can no longer accept another element while remaining feasible, or all elements have been presented (whichever occurs first).
    • The gambler measures her performance against an all-knowing prophet, who always collects the feasible set of elements with maximum reward. The objective is to design a policy for the gambler such that she collects, in expectation, a constant fraction of the prophet's expected reward.
    • Even when the gambler knows the distributions of all rewards, she cannot hope to obtain an expected reward of more than half of the prophet's expected reward. This can be seen, for instance, by considering the objective of collecting a single item, in the case where the first presented reward is deterministically 1, and the second reward is 0 w.p. 1-1/n, and n w.p. 1/n. In this case, the gambler (no matter her policy) can never obtain expected reward greater than 1, but the prophet collects a reward of 2-1/n.
    • Remarkably, even under general matroid constraints, KW'12 showed tht there exists a policy which collects in expectation half of the prophet's expected reward, assuming all reward distributions are known in advance.
    • However, assuming full distributional knowledge is a strong assumption. What if the gambler only had access to a single sample from each reward distribution, instead of full distributional access? It turns out that this setting is intimately related to the secretary problem (a related problem in optimal stopping theory, where the rewards are adversarially chosen, but the order is uniformly random). In fact, there is a black-box reduction by AKW'14 which allows one to obtain policies for the single-sample prophet inequality problem from a large class of policies for the secretary problem (these are so-called order-oblivious secretary policies).
    • This reduction comes at a cost, and, in particular, one cannot hope to obtain half of the prophets expected reward directly through this reduction. However, practically all known single-sample prophet inequalities to date are through this reduction.
    • However, RWW'20 showed that, given only a single sample from each distribution, the single-choice prophet inequality can be solved optimally! In fact, their policy is remarkably simple — accept the first reward exceeding the largest observed sample value, if such a reward exists.

    Summary:
    • Taking inspiration from these recent results for the single-choice single-sample prophet inequality (SSPI), we ask how to obtain improved SSPIs without relying on the black-box reduction of AKW'14.
    • Our first contribution is a general framework for analyzing the competitiveness SSPI policies against a greedy-like prophet solution. Using this framework, we obtain the first SSPI for general (non-bipartite) matching environments, as well as improved competitive ratios for transversal and truncated partition matroids.
    • Our second contribution is inspired by the observation that many secretary policies for matroids decompose the problem into independent rank-1 instances. We provide a meta-theorem that allows us to obtain improved competitive guarantees (most by a factor of 2) for most of the matroids considered in AKW'14 which satisfy this decomposition property, leveraging the recent improvements of RWW'20.

  • Mix and Match: An Optimistic Tree-Search Approach for Learning Models from Mixture Distributions
    Matthew Faw, Rajat Sen, Karthikeyan Shanmugam, Constantine Caramanis, and Sanjay Shakkottai.
    Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems, NeurIPS'20 (virtual).

    Summary:
    • We introduce a new framework to handle distribution shift, assuming access to a small validation dataset. Our assumption on distribution shift is more general than covariate shift, and accommodates latent variables.
    • Our assumptions naturally motivate formulating the problem in the continuous-armed bandit framework and using an optimistic tree-search algorithm to account for distribution shift.
    • Our analysis requires high-probability bounds on the error of the final iterate of SGD. We prove new bounds on the error of the final SGD iterate that do not require a global stochastic gradient bound!
    • We validate our theoretical results on three Kaggle datasets (an Allstate insurance dataset, a wine ratings dataset, and Amazon employee access dataset). The code to run the experiments and create the datasets can be found here.
    • All experiments were run on a Kubernetes cluster in Google Cloud. Code to create the experimental setup is avaliable here.
