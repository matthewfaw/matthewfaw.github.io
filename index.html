About Me

I am a second-year Ph.D. student in the ECE department at The University of Texas at Austin, where I am very fortunate to be advised by Sanjay Shakkottai and Constantine Caramanis.

Prior to joining UT Austin, I spent four fun years at Duke University, where I graduated with majors in Electrical & Computer Engineering, Computer Science, and Mathematics. During my time there, I had the great fortune of working with Ben Lee on designing economically sustainable algorithms for datacenter-level computational sprinting, Richard Fair on designing digital microfluidic devices capable of manipulating cells within a droplet, and Nick Buchler on using CRISPR technologies to create cells which perform logical operations.

Outside of school, I've also spent time in industry. During the summer of 2016, I was a software engineering intern at MathWorks on the Stateflow Semantics team, where I developed a new code generation framework. Between undergrad and grad school, I also spent a year working as a software engineer at a healthcare software startup called Verato, where I co-developed a continuous integration platform on Kubernetes and wrote code to maintain and update the identity data in the company's main database.

Research Interests

My interests include online learning, optimization (both convex and non-convex), adaptive data analysis, and transfer learning. Lately, I've been using ideas from convex optimization and bandits to develop algorithms to correct for distribution shift. I've recently discovered that convex optimization and continuous-armed bandit setups provide a really useful framework for studying covariate shift (and its generalizations), and believe that optimization and online learning will provide useful approaches to studying and designing algorithms with theoretical guarantees for a wide range of problems in transfer learning.

Selected Papers (Show all):

  • Mix and Match: An Optimistic Tree-Search Approach for Learning Models from Mixture Distributions
    Matthew Faw, Rajat Sen, Karthikeyan Shanmugam, Constantine Caramanis, and Sanjay Shakkottai.
    In submission.
    Summary:
    • We introduce a new framework to handle distribution shift, assuming access to a small validation dataset. Our assumption on distribution shift is more general than covariate shift, and accommodates latent variables.
    • Our assumptions naturally motivate formulating the problem in the continuous-armed bandit framework and using an optimistic tree-search algorithm to account for distribution shift.
    • Our analysis requires high-probability bounds on the error of the final iterate of SGD. We prove new bounds on the error of the final SGD iterate that do not require a global stochastic gradient bound!
    • We validate our theoretical results on three Kaggle datasets (an Allstate insurance dataset, a wine ratings dataset, and Amazon employee access dataset). The code to run the experiments and create the datasets can be found here.
    • All experiments were run on a Kubernetes cluster in Google Cloud. Code to create the experimental setup is avaliable here.
